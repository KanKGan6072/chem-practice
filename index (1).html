Acids & Bases

Theory, nomenclature, and active recall.

Definition 01
Arrhenius
The most restrictive definition — aqueous solutions only.

Acid: produces H⁺ ions in water.
Base: produces OH⁻ ions in water.
Acid example
HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻
Hydrochloric acid
Base example
NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻
Sodium hydroxide
Limitation: cannot explain why NH₃ acts as a base — it releases no OH⁻.
Definition 02
Brønsted–Lowry
Broader — doesn't require water. Based on proton (H⁺) transfer.

Acid: proton donor — gives away H⁺.
Base: proton acceptor — receives H⁺.
Proton transfer
HCl + H₂O → H₃O⁺ + Cl⁻
HCl = acid, H₂O = base
Conjugate pairs
Acid loses H⁺ → conjugate base
Base gains H⁺ → conjugate acid
HCl/Cl⁻ and H₂O/H₃O⁺
Explains NH₃ as a base (accepts H⁺ to form NH₄⁺). Water is amphoteric — it can be both acid and base.
Definition 03
Lewis
The broadest definition — no protons required. Based on electron pair transfer.

Acid: electron pair acceptor — has an empty orbital.
Base: electron pair donor — has a lone pair.
Lewis acid accepts e⁻ pair
BF₃ + NH₃ → BF₃NH₃
BF₃ = Lewis acid (empty p orbital)
Lewis base donates e⁻ pair
NH₃ = Lewis base
Lone pair on N donated to BF₃
No H⁺ transfer at all
Every Brønsted–Lowry acid is also a Lewis acid, but not vice versa.
Summary
Hierarchy
Lewis ⊃ Brønsted–Lowry ⊃ Arrhenius

Arrhenius is most restrictive (aqueous only, H⁺/OH⁻). Brønsted–Lowry extends to any proton transfer. Lewis covers any electron pair transfer — no protons required at all.
Naming rules
Acid Nomenclature
Binary acids (H + nonmetal, no oxygen):
→ hydro___ic acid
HCl = hydrochloric acid  ·  HBr = hydrobromic acid

Oxyacids (H + oxyanion):
→ Ion ending in -ate → acid ending in -ic acid
→ Ion ending in -ite → acid ending in -ous acid
HNO₃ (nitrate → nitric acid)  ·  HNO₂ (nitrite → nitrous acid)

Bases: metal + hydroxide — standard ionic naming.
NaOH = sodium hydroxide  ·  Ca(OH)₂ = calcium hydroxide
